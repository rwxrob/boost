# Typing speed matters much less than you think

For decades, "learn to type fast" was treated as a prerequisite for working in tech. Typing courses, WPM tests, and the assumption that productivity was bottlenecked by how fast your fingers moved.

That assumption is no longer true. Typing speed is one of the least important skills you can develop right now.

## What changed

Three things happened at roughly the same time:

**AI writes most of the text.** When you work with a coding agent, the majority of what gets produced — code, documentation, commit messages, boilerplate — comes from the model, not your keyboard. You're directing, reviewing, and correcting, not transcribing.

**Autocomplete fills in the rest.** Even when you are typing, tools like GitHub Copilot complete your thoughts before you finish them. The actual number of keystrokes required to produce working code has dropped by an order of magnitude.

**Voice input works now.** Tools like Superwhisper transcribe speech to text accurately enough that many prompts, messages, and even code comments are faster to speak than type. A 200-word prompt takes about 90 seconds to speak and several minutes to type.

## What typing is still good for

Typing isn't dead — it's just less central than it used to be.

You still want to be comfortable enough to:

- Enter short terminal commands without hunting and pecking
- Make quick corrections to AI output without it feeling painful
- Navigate a text editor at a reasonable pace
- Type passwords, search queries, and one-liners without frustration

"Comfortable" doesn't mean fast. It means not being blocked by your own keyboard.

## What actually matters now

The skills that bottleneck productivity today have nothing to do with fingers:

- **Clarity of thought** — can you describe what you want precisely enough for AI to produce it?
- **Reading and evaluation** — can you scan output quickly and spot what's wrong?
- **Systems thinking** — do you understand the system well enough to give good instructions?
- **Prompt crafting** — can you iterate on a prompt until you get something useful?

None of these require fast typing. All of them require clear thinking.

## A word on touch typing

If you don't touch type at all, spending a week or two learning the home row position is worth it — not for speed, but so you can look at the screen while you type. Hunting and pecking while staring at the keyboard splits your attention in a way that slows down thinking, not just fingers.

But the goal is fluency, not speed. Getting to 40-50 WPM with proper touch typing technique is enough. Chasing 100+ WPM is not a good use of your learning time anymore.

## The bottom line

Don't let slow typing stop you from starting. The bottleneck in tech work today is thinking, not typing. Use voice input for long prompts, autocomplete for code, and save your keystrokes for the things that actually need them.
