# Typing speed matters much less than you think

For decades, "learn to type fast" was treated as a prerequisite for working in tech. Typing courses, WPM tests, and the assumption that productivity was bottlenecked by how fast your fingers moved.

That assumption is no longer true. Typing speed is one of the least important skills you can develop right now.

## What changed

Three things happened at roughly the same time:

**AI writes most of the text.** When you work with a coding agent, the majority of what gets produced — code, documentation, commit messages, boilerplate — comes from the model, not your keyboard. You're directing, reviewing, and correcting, not transcribing.

**Autocomplete fills in the rest.** Even when you are typing, tools like GitHub Copilot complete your thoughts before you finish them. The actual number of keystrokes required to produce working code has dropped by an order of magnitude.

**Voice input works now.** Tools like [Superwhisper](https://superwhisper.com) transcribe speech to text accurately enough that many prompts, messages, and even code comments are faster to speak than type. A 200-word prompt takes about 90 seconds to speak and several minutes to type.

## What typing is still good for

Typing isn't dead — it's just less central than it used to be.

You still want to be comfortable enough to:

- Enter short terminal commands without hunting and pecking
- Make quick corrections to AI output without it feeling painful
- Navigate a text editor at a reasonable pace
- Type passwords, search queries, and one-liners without frustration

"Comfortable" doesn't mean fast. It means not being blocked by your own keyboard.

## What actually matters now

The skills that bottleneck productivity today have nothing to do with fingers:

- **Clarity of thought** — can you describe what you want precisely enough for AI to produce it?
- **Reading and evaluation** — can you scan output quickly and spot what's wrong?
- **Systems thinking** — do you understand the system well enough to give good instructions?
- **Prompt crafting** — can you iterate on a prompt until you get something useful?

None of these require fast typing. All of them require clear thinking.

## Stop optimizing your editor instead of building things

The typing speed trap has a close cousin: spending weeks configuring NeoVim, chasing the perfect `.vimrc`, watching YouTube videos about someone else's Lua plugin setup, and endlessly tweaking keybindings. The same pattern shows up with Emacs, shell prompts, and terminal color schemes.

None of this is building anything. It feels productive because you are learning and making decisions, but the output is a config file that helps you edit text marginally faster. Meanwhile, the person next to you with a stock VS Code install and [Superwhisper](https://superwhisper.com) is shipping features by talking to their AI agent.

Editor configuration is a form of procrastination that disguises itself as professionalism. It signals depth to other enthusiasts, but employers don't care how many plugins you have. They care whether you can deliver working software.

This doesn't mean editors don't matter. Pick one that works, learn it well enough to not fight it, and move on. The diminishing returns on editor configuration set in fast — usually within the first few hours. Everything after that is hobby time, not career investment.

## Learn tools that multiply your output instead

[Superwhisper](https://superwhisper.com) is a good example of a tool that actually changes how fast you work. Speaking a 200-word prompt takes about 90 seconds. Typing it takes several minutes. Over the course of a day spent working with AI agents, voice input saves hours — not because it is a novelty, but because it removes a genuine bottleneck between your brain and the machine. Consider grabbing the [lifetime license](https://superwhisper.com) ($249 direct from the site, not the App Store) while it is still available — once voice input becomes mainstream, deals like that tend to disappear.

The same logic applies to learning `git` deeply, understanding containers, writing good prompts, and knowing how to read code. These are tools that compound. A faster NeoVim config does not compound. It plateaus almost immediately.

If you have an hour to invest in getting better at tech work, spend it on something that changes the shape of your workflow — not something that shaves milliseconds off keystrokes you shouldn't be making in the first place.

## A word on touch typing

If you don't touch type at all, spending a week or two learning the home row position is worth it — not for speed, but so you can look at the screen while you type. Hunting and pecking while staring at the keyboard splits your attention in a way that slows down thinking, not just fingers.

But the goal is fluency, not speed. Getting to 40-50 WPM with proper touch typing technique is enough. Chasing 100+ WPM is not a good use of your learning time anymore.

## The bottom line

Don't let slow typing or an unoptimized editor stop you from starting. The bottleneck in tech work today is thinking, not typing — and certainly not having the perfect NeoVim setup. Use voice input for long prompts, autocomplete for code, and save your keystrokes for the things that actually need them. Invest your learning time in tools that change what you can do, not tools that make you marginally faster at what you already do.
