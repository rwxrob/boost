# Your agentic AI template is the new dotfiles

A decade ago, the first thing a serious developer did when setting up a new machine was clone their dotfiles repo.
Their shell config, their editor settings, their aliases, their keybindings — all of it portable, version-controlled, and ready to go in minutes.
It said: this person has been doing this long enough to have strong opinions about their environment, and they maintain those opinions deliberately.

In 2026, the equivalent signal is your agentic AI template.

## What dotfiles said about you

Dotfiles were never just about convenience.
They were a statement of craft.
A well-maintained dotfiles repo told anyone who looked that you understood your tools deeply, that you had iterated on your workflow over time, and that you took your environment seriously.
Interviewers noticed.
Teammates noticed.
It was a proxy for how you approached everything else.

## What an agentic AI template says about you today

An agentic AI template is a starting point for any project that uses AI agents — a repo you own that includes a structured `AGENTS.md`, your conventions, your rules, your architecture patterns, and your current stack.
When you start a new project, you fork it.
When you learn something new about working with agents, you update it.

Maintaining one says:

- You work with AI tools deeply enough to have developed real opinions about how to structure that relationship
- You understand that context is the bottleneck — the agent is only as good as what you tell it
- You have iterated on your workflow and codified what works
- You treat your AI tooling as infrastructure, not as a search engine you occasionally consult

That is exactly the signal dotfiles sent a decade ago.

## The practical reasons

Beyond signaling, there are hard practical reasons to maintain your own template.

**Context is everything.**
Every time you start a new project without a solid `AGENTS.md`, you spend the first part of every session re-explaining your conventions, your preferences, and your current state.
That is waste.
A good template eliminates that from day one.

**Consistency across projects.**
When your rules live in a template, every project you create inherits them automatically.
Your commit style, your file naming conventions, your secrets policy, your code style — consistent from the first commit.

**Portability.**
Your template goes with you.
New job, new team, new machine — you clone your template and your entire way of working with AI agents comes with you.

**Onboarding speed.**
If you ever work with others, your `AGENTS.md` becomes the fastest possible onboarding document.
A new collaborator — human or agent — can read it and immediately understand how the project works and what the rules are.

## The employment angle

Hiring managers in 2026 are starting to ask the same questions about AI workflow that they used to ask about version control hygiene.
Do you maintain your environment deliberately?
Do you have strong opinions about how you work?
Can you show me something that reflects how you think?

A public, well-maintained agentic AI template answers all three.
It is not just a tool — it is a portfolio artifact.
It demonstrates that you have moved past copy-pasting from chat windows and into deliberate, repeatable, opinionated AI-assisted development.
