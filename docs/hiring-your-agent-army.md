# Hiring Your Agent Army

In 2026, when you hire someone for a tech role, you are not just hiring a person. You are hiring a person and everything they can orchestrate.

The question is: how much can they orchestrate?

## The wrong question

For years, tech interviews have asked things like: "How many lines of code did you write last year?" or "How many hours did you spend on this project?" These questions made sense when output was roughly proportional to individual effort.

Then came AI.

Now some interviewers ask: "How many tokens did you use last year?" It is a more interesting signal, but it is still missing the point. Token count is a proxy. It tells you someone touched AI tools, but not whether they used them well.

## The better question

Ask instead: **"What AI tools do you use, how, and when?"**

That single question reveals everything. The answers span a spectrum so wide it might as well be measuring different species of engineer.

At one end: someone who pastes a Stack Overflow question into ChatGPT, reads the response, and manually copies the code into their editor. AI as a slightly smarter search engine.

At the other end: someone dictating architectural decisions to an agent from their phone while driving to work, spinning up parallel agents to research, prototype, and review — arriving at the office with a pull request already in review.

Both people "use AI." But they are not doing the same thing.

## What the how reveals

The *how* tells you the depth of integration.

Is AI a tab they switch to, or is it woven into every moment of their workflow? Do they trust it enough to delegate? Do they understand its failure modes well enough to verify its output? Can they decompose a complex problem across multiple agents and synthesize the results?

A few questions that surface this:

- Show me something you built with agents that you couldn't have done alone.
- When did an agent confidently give you wrong output, and how did you catch it?
- How do you decide when to use a cheap model versus a capable one?
- What can't you delegate to agents yet — and why?

That last one is the most revealing. It exposes self-awareness about where human judgment is still irreplaceable versus where someone is just being cautious or afraid to let go.

## The real signal

You are not looking for the person who uses AI the most. You are looking for the person for whom AI is ambient — not a special occasion, but the default mode of working.

The person who only reaches for AI when stuck is still working the old way. The person who has restructured their entire workflow around AI augmentation is operating at a different level entirely.

When you hire in 2026, you are not just hiring skills. You are hiring a system. The question worth asking is whether that system is a bicycle or a rocket ship — and whether the person riding it actually knows how to fly.
